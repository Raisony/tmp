\chapter{Introduction}
Uncertainty in machine learning systems encompasses the various factors that can undermine the robustness and efficiency. Leading machine learning vendors such as Google, Meta, Alibaba have reported and highlighted that uncertainty in their industrial-scale machine learning systems can cause service interruptions and quality degradation as well as potential revenue losses~\cite{dixit2021silent, hochschild2021cores, wang2023understanding, he2023understanding, HC2023-K1}. Uncertainty in machine learning systems requires significant effort to mitigate its impact where the system may even further compromise the efficiency to ensure robustness. Therefore, it becomes clear that uncertainty is one prohibitive obstacle on the path towards efficient and robust machine learning systems. In this chapter, we first introduce the diverse sources of uncertainty in machine learning systems that include hardware and architecture as well as software, application and data. Then, we highlight the challenges of addressing uncertainty from the perspective of characterization and exposure of uncertainty across different layers in machine learning systems. We correspondingly present our proposed solutions to combat against uncertainty and advance towards robust and efficient machine learning systems via diverse joint-optimization efforts.


\section{The Diverse Sources of Uncertainty}
\subsection{Uncertainty from Hardware and Architecture}

\textbf{Errors from Compute Units} As transistor sizes continue to shrink into the deep nanometer scale, microelectronic circuits are becoming increasingly susceptible to uncertainties caused by variability in microelectronic processes~\cite{gupta2012underdesigned}. Compute units, which are fundamental building blocks of machine learning systems, are particularly vulnerable to these variability, which can stem from sources such as operating conditions, manufacturing inconsistencies, or aging~\cite{gupta2012underdesigned}. One immediate consequence of such variations is the delay uncertainty, which can prevent circuits from meeting their timing specifications, leading to timing errors. Without adequate safeguards, these timing errors can significantly threaten the accuracy and reliability of compute units. Another notable source of errors in compute units is the use of approximate logic. Approximate logic involves circuit designs that trade computational accuracy for improvements in power consumption, latency, or area. Previous studies have shown the effectiveness of designing approximate compute units, such as inexact multipliers, to enhance efficiency in compute-intensive systems~\cite{shafique2015low, venkatachalam2017design, mrazek2017evoapprox8b, mannepalli2021novel}. However, while approximate logic offers significant benefits, it can also introduce errors that, if not properly managed, may degrade output quality or even cause system-level disruptions.

\textbf{Errors from Memory} Memory is another critical component of machine learning systems, as it stores and provides access to large volumes of data, including model parameters. However, memory in these systems is also prone to errors. For instance, various beam tests have demonstrated that cosmic rays and radiation can induce soft transient errors in GPU memories~\cite{tiwari2015understanding, sullivan2021characterizing}. Additionally, manufacturing defects and device aging can lead to permanent faults, which are especially common in deep learning accelerators with systolic arrays~\cite{hanif2020dependable, zhao2022fsa}. As state-of-the-art models, such as large language models, demand ever-greater memory capacity and computational resources, it becomes increasingly important to understand the impact of memory errors and to enhance fault tolerance in these systems to minimize the uncertainty.

\subsection{Uncertainty from Algorithm and Data}

\textbf{Model Optimizations} In machine learning, post-training optimization can further enhance model performance, reduce computational costs, and improve deployment efficiency. Techniques such as pruning and compression are used to reduce the size of large and dense networks, creating sparsity and improving memory efficiency~\cite{han2016eie}. Quantization techniques convert floating-point operations into fixed-point operations, which consume less energy during computation~\cite{lin2016fixed}. The lottery ticket hypothesis also posits that within a large, randomly initialized network, there exists a much smaller sub-network that, when trained in isolation, can achieve performance comparable to the original network~\cite{frankle2018lottery}. However, because optimization often involves approximations and modifications (such as parameter redistribution), it can introduce uncertainty into the system, leading to potential unexpected behaviors that must be carefully monitored and mitigated.

As to more aggressive model optimizations, researchers have also explored approximate computing, where model performance is intentionally traded for improved efficiency~\cite{zervakis2021approximate, castro2020axhls}, leveraging the inherent error tolerance of machine learning applications~\cite{st2014general, ma2021workload, baek2010green, venkataramani2014axnn, adelman2021faster}. This trade-off is typically implemented on resource-constrained machine learning platforms, which often have limited computational power, memory, or energy resources. However, without a deep understanding of how different approximations affect model behavior, there is a risk of excessive and unintended performance degradation, resulting in unacceptable output quality. Therefore, achieving a controlled balance between accuracy and efficiency, referred to as ``graceful'' approximation, is essential.

\textbf{Adversarial Attacks} With the deployment of machine learning systems in real-world applications, data emerges as another significant source of uncertainty. Malicious users can engineer input data with subtle perturbations that induce erroneous behaviors in models, known as adversarial attacks~\cite{madry2017towards}. These attacks vary based on the attackers' access to the machine learning model and the extent of information they can glean, which can be categorized into three distinct scenarios: white-box, gray-box, and black-box~\cite{ilyas2018black}. In the white-box scenario, the attacker possesses full access to the model, facilitating comprehensive information extraction during the inference process. Gray-box and black-box scenarios, which are more prevalent in practical settings, however offer limited access. In a gray-box context, the attacker gains partial information, such as soft labels, whereas in a black-box context, the attacker is restricted to inputting samples and observing output labels without insights into the internal mechanisms of the model. These adversarial attacks introduce a new layer of uncertainty into machine learning systems, thereby exacerbating the vulnerability of critical applications such as drones and autonomous vehicles~\cite{guo2018dlfuzz, pei2017deepxplore}.

\section{Addressing Uncertainty in Machine Learning Systems: Challenges and Our Solutions}
\subsection{Context-aware Uncertainty Modeling in Computing Systems}

To safeguard computing units from errors, it is crucial for designers to precisely comprehend and model these error behaviors. The most accurate method to capture the error dynamics of computing units is through logic simulation via design automation tool-chains and hardware simulators. Nonetheless, these simulators are not only costly and challenging to configure but also inefficient: Designers are required to construct the simulation flow, which involves writing test benches, supplying input stimuli, and conducting time-consuming logic simulations. Crucially, this procedure is neither user-friendly nor readily accessible to a broader audience, such as machine learning engineers, who might need to assess the application-level impact of compute errors but lack the necessary expertise in hardware design.

Therefore, many models are proposed to predict the correctness of arithmetic instructions~\cite{rahimi2012analysis, rahimi2014application, constantin2015exploiting} or functional units~\cite{tziantzioulis2015b, rahimi2013hierarchically, sartori2011stochastic, esmaeilzadeh2012architecture}. For example, instruction-level models~\cite{rahimi2012analysis, rahimi2014application, constantin2015exploiting} predict timing errors based on instruction types. B-hive~\cite{tziantzioulis2015b} and HFG~\cite{rahimi2013hierarchically} both use machine learning methods to predict timing violations of functional units under dynamic variations. Many error metrics have also been established, such as mean square error (MSE), error distance (ED), mean error distance (MED), normalized mean error distance (NMED), relative error distance (RED), and mean absolute error (MAE)~\cite{liu2014low, maheshwari2015design, vcevska2017approximating}.

While current error models are beneficial for evaluating general error metrics, they often fail to account for the effects of contexts such as input data. Typically, these metrics are derived from offline analyses; however, varying conditions such as operational conditions and input data can provoke significantly different behaviors in logic errors, even within the same compute unit. Developing context-aware error models presents a significant challenge due to the vast input space. For instance, a multiplier handling two 16-bit operands formulates an input space of $2^{16 + 16}$. This complexity raises uncertainty about how different inputs might activate various circuit paths and dynamically induce diverse error behaviors, which is an NP-complete problem~\cite{mcgeer2012integrating}.

We are thus motivated to employ machine learning, which is capable of predicting errors in functional units due to its proficiency in modeling patterns and their relationships within large datasets. This enables learning the input-output behaviors without the necessity to brute-force every input possible for the compute unit. This method is significantly faster and simpler in comparison to hardware simulations. Once developed, these models can further be generalized to forecast errors across novel inputs and unexplored contexts, rendering their versatility and efficiency for characterizing uncertainty in computing systems.

\subsection{Cross-layer Exposure of Uncertainty}

Exposing and managing uncertainty in machine learning systems is a complex challenge due to the diverse ways in which errors propagate and impact different layers of the system. At the hardware level, even minor bit errors, particularly in critical regions such as the exponent field of floating-point numbers, can lead to significant deviations that disrupt the entire system, resulting in issues like invalid predictions or unstable training processes. On the other hand, errors in less critical areas may instead accumulate gradually, eventually degrading model performance as well. The robustness of machine learning models and architectural components also varies significantly in response to these uncertainties. For example, in deep recommender systems, certain components like embedding tables can withstand higher error rates with minimal performance loss, whereas others, such as multilayer perceptrons, are far more sensitive, exhibiting substantial performance degradation even at lower error rates. This variability complicates the task of effectively managing and mitigating uncertainty throughout the machine learning system, necessitating tailored approaches for each layer and component.

To address these challenges, we have developed a cross-layer fault injection framework that enables fast and accurate emulation of uncertainty within machine learning systems. This framework offers several key advantages over existing fault injection methods: First, it efficiently performs error injections on model parameters across different layers, allowing flexible and rapid injections whether targeting a single bit in a parameter or an entire layer. Second, it is specifically tailored for PyTorch, one of the most widely used machine learning frameworks, and does not require significant memory or latency overhead as compared to the related efforts~\cite{mahmoud2020pytorchfi, reagen2018ares, chen2019binfi, mahmoud2022goldeneye}. This ensures that the error injection process remains efficient, even for sophisticated models such as large language models. Furthermore, the framework is highly adaptable, supporting a broad range of model architectures and components, and provides interfaces for implementing or integrating custom error mitigation strategies with minimal dependencies beyond PyTorch. This powerful framework enables a more detailed characterization of the impact of uncertainty on different layers within a machine learning model, which is particularly beneficial for models with complex architectures, such as deep recommender systems. Ultimately, it facilitates the joint optimization of performance, robustness, and efficiency in these systems.

\subsection{Joint Optimization of Performance, Robustness and Efficiency}

While machine learning applications exhibit a certain tolerance to uncertainty, striving for (near-)zero error rates in hardware can lead to significant performance or latency overhead due to the additional redundancy required~\cite{jiao2023brief}. Although various methods exist to combat uncertainty, many focus exclusively on either robustness or efficiency, often neglecting the other, or are too ad-hoc to enable flexible optimization. For instance, error correction code (ECC) is a widely used method for identifying and mitigating hardware errors, such as single-bit and double-bit errors; however, employing ECC for HBM2 memory on compute-class GPUs requires about 12.5\% redundancy~\cite{chen2018configurable}. ECC can also introduce a substantial performance penalty in AI processing latency~\cite{jouppi2021ten}. Recently, algorithm-based fault tolerance (ABFT) has been evaluated on the MLP and embedding components of deep recommender systems (DRS) models to address soft errors~\cite{li2022efficient}, but it assumes error correction is achieved by simply re-executing the impacted operations, a method that becomes impractical as the scale of errors increases. Furthermore, there has been limited effort in optimizing configurations within approximate computing to achieve graceful performance degradation, balancing the desired trade-off between performance and efficiency.

Our solution involves a holistic approach that jointly considers performance, robustness, and efficiency, enabling comprehensive and flexible optimization tailored to the specific system and application requirements. In the context of approximate computing, for instance, we explore a finer-grained configuration strategy that accounts for the significant variations across layers and components within a machine learning model, thereby achieving higher efficiency with minimal impact on accuracy. In the emerging field of hyperdimensional computing -- a bio-inspired computational paradigm focused on efficiency, we investigate its robustness against uncertainty from adversarial attacks, an area that has not yet been thoroughly explored. For deep learning models, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation of the impact of silent data corruptions and propose an early detection and mitigation scheme that is reconfigurable based on performance and efficiency needs. Our broad joint-optimization efforts across diverse research areas and applications pave the way for the next generation of robust and efficient machine learning systems.

\section{Organization of Dissertation}

This dissertation aims to outline a comprehensive, cross-layer study to accurately characterize, evaluate, and mitigate uncertainty in machine learning systems, providing systematic approaches for the joint optimization of performance, efficiency, and robustness. Specifically, we begin by enhancing the characterization of errors, faults, and other uncertainties by incorporating richer context, such as operating conditions and historical input, to achieve a more relevant, accurate, and rapid assessment of robustness in machine learning. We then explore robustness across different layers of a machine learning system to thoroughly investigate the impact of various models, algorithms, architectures, and uncertainty manifestations. By deeply characterizing, modeling, and exposing robustness at multiple levels within a machine learning system, we conduct a series of case studies across a diverse range of applications, including computer vision, recommender systems, and conversational AI, to jointly optimize the performance, efficiency, and robustness of these emerging machine learning applications.

The organization of this dissertation is as follows:

\begin{itemize} 

    \item In \cref{sec:c2modeling}, we aim to model uncertainty in machine learning architectures with richer context, providing a more pertinent, accurate, and rapid characterization of uncertainty. 
    \item In \cref{sec:c3exposure}, we expose the modeling of uncertainty to machine learning applications, directly connecting robustness with application performance and efficiency metrics, enabling further system-wide optimization. 
    \item In \cref{sec:c4optimization}, we leverage our modeling and exposure efforts to explore the balance between performance, efficiency, and robustness through joint optimization. We examine a broad selection of models, algorithms, system architectures, and uncertainty manifestations, providing a systematic approach for designers to identify optimal trade-offs based on their design requirements. 
    \item In \cref{sec:c5con}, we conclude this dissertation by summarizing our efforts and contributions, and by highlighting the potential future works of the discussed research directions. 
\end{itemize}
